      03 · Mechanism

      Why 6 breaths per minute is not arbitrary

      When you exhale slowly, the vagus nerve actively brakes your heart. At roughly 6 breaths per minute — your resonance frequency — the natural oscillations of heart rate, blood pressure, and respiration align into a single amplified wave. This trains the baroreflex, which over weeks raises baseline vagal tone. Higher vagal tone means lower resting heart rate, lower inflammation, better sleep, and the ability to downshift when the threat has passed. Every protocol below either stimulates this directly (breathwork, cold) or removes what suppresses it (sleep, alcohol, overtraining).

      The two levers

      • Stimulate — breathwork, cold, zone 2, sauna, biofeedback
      • Remove suppressors — alcohol, late meals, blue light, overtraining, fragmented sleep
      04 · Protocols

      The seven protocols, ranked by leverage

      Stress/burnout context. Top three deliver most of the recovery; the rest are accelerants.

      01 Highest leverage

      Resonance breathing — 4 in, 6 out

      The single most evidence-backed intervention. Direct vagal activation, immediate effect, compounds for 4–8 weeks.

      Dose5–20 min/day
      Rate~6 breaths/min
      PatternInhale 4s, exhale 6s
      Felt bySession 1
      Baseline shiftWeek 4
      Best timingMorning + pre-sleep

      The extended exhale is the active ingredient — it lengthens the phase when the vagus is firing. At 4:6 you're in parasympathetic dominance 60% of every cycle.

      02Highest leverage

      Sleep architecture, not just duration

      Window7–9 hrs, same time daily
      Bedroom temp15–19°C / 59–66°F
      Last meal≥3 hrs before bed
      Magnesium200–400 mg glycinate

      Morning HRV is taken during the deep-sleep parasympathetic window. Anything fragmenting that window — alcohol, late food, heat, light — directly suppresses your reading.

      03Highest leverage

      Cut evening alcohol

      Effect sizeOften the largest single suppressor
      MechanismSympathetic spike during sleep
      Test2 dry weeks, watch baseline
      ThresholdEven 1–2 drinks measurable

      If your daily HRV is volatile and you drink at night, this is almost certainly the variable. The dose-response is brutal and individual.

      04Strong accelerant

      Zone 2 cardio, 3–4×/week

      Dose30–45 min, conversational pace
      Heart rate60–70% of max
      ModesBrisk walk, cycling, swim
      AvoidDaily high-intensity in burnout

      Builds parasympathetic capacity without hammering recovery. In burnout states, hard intervals often lower HRV for days; zone 2 raises it.

      05Strong accelerant

      Brief cold exposure

      Start30–60s cold shower finish
      Progress to2–3 min at 10–15°C
      Frequency2–3× per week
      MechanismDiving reflex → vagal rebound

      Acute sympathetic spike followed by a 20–60% parasympathetic rebound in the hours after. Skip if it leaves you wired or depleted — not everyone responds.

      06Optional

      Sauna with cool-down

      Dose15–20 min at 70–90°C
      Frequency2–4× per week
      Pair withCool shower after
      BonusAll-cause mortality benefit

      Hormetic stress similar to exercise. The post-sauna cool-down amplifies parasympathetic rebound the same way cold does, with a gentler entry.

      07Optional

      HRV biofeedback (optional kit)

      ToolsElite HRV, HRV4Training, Oura/Whoop
      UseFind your personal resonance rate
      Worth it ifYou're a data person
      Skippable ifYou'll do the practice anyway

      Tightens the feedback loop. Useful for finding your exact resonance frequency (likely between 4.5–6.5 bpm), but the benefits come from the breathing — not the device.

      05 · Trajectory

      What to expect, week by week

      Day 1

      Single 5-min breathwork session produces measurable HRV bump within minutes. Don't expect the bump to persist yet.

      Week 1

      Subjective shift first: easier sleep onset, less reactive to stressors, longer fuse. Baseline HRV usually still noisy.

      Week 2–4

      Morning HRV trend starts climbing 5–15% if practice is consistent and alcohol is cut. Daily volatility narrows.

      Week 4–8

      Baseline shift visible on a 30-day chart. RHR drops 2–5 bpm. Inflammation markers (CRP, IL-6) often improve.

      Week 8–12

      10–30% HRV gain typical for consistent practitioners. New baseline holds as long as practice + sleep hygiene continue.

      07 · Suppressors

      What flattens HRV fast

      In a stress-recovery state, removing these often outperforms adding new protocols.

      Evening alcohol

      The single largest suppressor for most people. Even 1–2 drinks measurably blunts overnight HRV.

      Late heavy meals

      Digestion competes with parasympathetic recovery. Cut off intake 3 hours before sleep.

      Daily high-intensity training

      In burnout, HIIT and heavy lifting suppress HRV for 24–72 hrs. Use sparingly until baseline recovers.

      Inconsistent sleep timing

      Variable bed/wake times produce volatile HRV even at adequate duration. Anchor the wake time first.

      Caffeine after 2pm

      Half-life of 5–6 hours. Even if you fall asleep, it fragments deep sleep and the parasympathetic window.

      Phone in bed

      Blue light suppresses melatonin; the content keeps the SNS engaged. Both directions hurt.

      08 · Interpretation

      Reading your data correctly

      ±10%

      Day-to-day noise. Normal. Ignore single-day swings within this band.

      −20–30%

      Sustained 3+ days. Real recovery signal. Back off training, prioritize sleep, audit alcohol.

      30 days

      Trend window. The only meaningful chart. Single nights are weather; the 30-day line is climate.

      You vs you

      Never compare. Healthy adult HRV ranges from the 20s to 200+ ms. Your own baseline is the only reference.
